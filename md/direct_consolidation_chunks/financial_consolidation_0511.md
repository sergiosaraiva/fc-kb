## Consolidation Impact:
- Year 1: Two companies (P and A)
- Year 2: Three companies (P, A, and B)
- Intercompany receivables/payables exist
- Cash flow statement must reflect all transactions


Let's take knowledge of the main events of this group.

- In Year 1, nothing special happened, excepted the existence of intercompany receivables and payables between both companies  
In Year 2, company P sells to  3rd  Parties  20%  of its participation in company A.  
- The cash received gives the financial means for P to acquire  70%  of a new company B.  
Moreover,  \mathbb{P}  increases its capital in order to follow an increase in capital in company B.  
All these transactions take place on the first days of January, Year 2.  
- After their general annual meetings closing Year 1, both companies P and A pay dividends. When negotiating the disposal of  20%  of A shares, there was an agreement for P to keep the rights to dividends for these  20% .

All these transactions are clearly reflected in the statutory accounts.
